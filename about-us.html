Integrative Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

CBT is a term used to describe a type of therapeutic treatment that incorporates strategies from both cognitive and behavioural therapies, as well as other scientifically proven evidence-based treatments. Panic, social phobia, bipolar disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, generalised anxiety and rumination, eating disorders, marital distress, anger, chronic pain, and trauma have all been shown to be highly effective with these treatments. CBT aids in the development of effective coping skills.

According to the cognitive therapy model, our thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are all intertwined and affect one another. Identifying unhelpful and distorted thinking, testing and modifying beliefs, and developing skills to distance oneself from one's thoughts are all examples of cognitive interventions. The premise is that our core beliefs and stories about ourselves that we developed as children continue to influence our current behaviours. These stories act as filters, distorting our perceptions and interactions with others and potentially leading to self-fulfilling prophecies in relationships. Cognitive interventions help people understand how their early childhood conditioning is affecting their current relationships and influencing their behaviours. The goal is to teach people how to recognise the link between their thoughts, feelings, and behaviours in order to develop behavioural flexibility.

Impulse Control & Emotional Dysregulation

Emotional dysregulation is defined as a problem with controlling, regulating, and tolerating one's emotions and emotional reactions – impulse control. If you have trouble regulating your emotions, it's possible that you grew up in an environment where your feelings were dismissed or ignored. Your emotions may have been slapped in the face, and you may have been blamed or chastised for your emotional reactions. Growing up in a household where feelings and needs aren't acknowledged can lead to confusion and doubt about your own emotional experiences, such as thoughts, feelings, needs, yearnings, and urges.

What are the signs?

Angry outbursts
Rapid fluctuations in mood
Impulsive decision making
Impulsive reactions
Chronic feelings of deprivation or emptiness
Difficulty managing emotional reactions
Difficulty recognizing and labeling emotions
Difficulty managing emotional responses in triggering situations

Relationship and Communication Problems

Do you have a tendency to lash out at others? Do you let your feelings dictate how you interact with others? Do you have trouble seeing things from other people's perspectives? In your relationship, do you feel stuck, hopeless, and alone? Do you find it difficult to get your needs met? You're not on your own. Even though we all have to interact with other people on a daily basis, many of us struggle with interpersonal relationships.

What are the signs?

Trouble getting your needs met in relationships
Difficulty feeling connected
Types of partners that are not good for you
Negative relationship patterns that occur repeatedly
Struggle with expressing feelings and needs
Difficulty setting clear boundaries
Trouble with managing anger
Toxic relationship dynamics
Tendency to withdraw or disconnect
Feelings of being trapped or ambivalent about relationships
Chronic feelings of dissatisfaction and loneliness

Work-Related Stress & Job Burnout

Burnout is a condition that occurs as a result of prolonged and excessive work-related stress. People often don't realise they're dealing with work-related stress until it's had a major negative impact on their relationships, quality of life, and physical health. It is common for people to experience some level of stress at work, but if you find yourself experiencing the symptoms listed below on a regular basis, it is critical that you begin exploring your options.

What are the signs?

Mental, emotional, and physical exhaustion
Irritability
Anger
Emotional reactivity
Loss of motivation
Feelings of apathy and hopelessness
Chronic feeling of inadequacy and failure
Frequent feelings of boredom and dissatisfaction

Procrastination & Indecision

Procrastination is not a flaw or a character flaw that must be overcome; it is a way of working that can be improved. Procrastination is a skill that can be learned.
The way we manage time and complete tasks is influenced by our personality types. In order to motivate and use time efficiently, some people benefit from having stricter time constraints and higher levels of anxiety. The key is to learn how to procrastinate effectively. Author John Perry explains how he uses "structured procrastination" in his strategy for effective procrastination.

What are the signs?

Chronic lateness
Poor time management
Putting off tasks
Difficulty meeting deadlines
Difficulty making decisions
Difficulty prioritizing and accomplishing tasks
Making excuses and distracting from completing important tasks
